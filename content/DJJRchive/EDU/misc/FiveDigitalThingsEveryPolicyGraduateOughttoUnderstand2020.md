# Five Tech Things  Every Policy Student Ought to Understand

Dan Ryan Zeroth Draft January 2020

# Networks

"Network" rolls off the modern tongue with deceptive familiarity.  Because they are ubiquitous, we think we understand what they are and why they are important. But we don't. But we should. We start this module by situating "network" among its conceptual siblings as a form of social organization, a technological topology, and a mathematical/computational concept and then learning some conceptual tools that will let us more clearly understand the "network" of digital network, communication networks, disease networks, deep learning networks, criminal networks, and biological networks.

# Encryption/Blockchain

How do machines keep secrets?  This module situates modern cryptography in the history of secret keeping and introduces students to the math and logic necessary to understand public key cryptography, hashing, and blockchain.

# Spatial Data

Everything is somewhere. Spatial data processing brings together the map, the inventory; when added to real time networks, we get remarkable capacity to link abstract models of what's going on in the world with what's going on in the world, both for tracking it and for stage-directing things from afar.  But what are the components of the "system" and how do they fit together?  That's what we will learn in this module.

# Vision

Understanding how computers see is important for two reasons.  First, the development of machine vision had a big impact on how machine learning is currently practiced.  When we understand the basics of how machine vision works, we understand a lot about how deep learning works. Second, one of the most important policy issues in the AI space today is facial recognition; in this module we lay the groundwork for what a policy expert needs to know about this topic.

# AI

What's behind the buzzword of the decade?  In this module we start with automation and statistics as the grandparents of machine learning and then learn the taxonomy of artificial intelligences before doing a deep dive on one type, reinforcement learning, as a means of being able to make sense of the "AI alignment problem."
