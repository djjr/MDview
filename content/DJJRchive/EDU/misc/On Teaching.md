I have taught in a variety of contexts - small liberal arts college, R1, law schools, school of information - and across a range of subjects - social and policy sciences, math and computer science, and design and innovation. Who I am now as a teacher is a cumulation of these experiences.
##### WHY
I teach because I enjoy it, all of it: the preparation, the implementation, the small dent it allows me to make in the world.  One dent is to help students to take themself seriously as a thinkers, to transform themselves from good student who excels at "what the teacher wants" to learner motivated by the thrill of mastery. A second dent is fostering a desire to be effective in the world and imparting the skills that requires. This, I suppose, is my teaching "philosophy."
##### WHENCE
My repertoire of teaching skills consists of practices and attitudes stolen from those who taught me. The mathematician Soo Bong Chae, taught me that a teacher is a farmer who tills and plants, waters, fertilizes and weeds, but then stands aside to let the sun do its work.  From sociologist Penny Rosel, I learned the sacredness of class time and the value of credibility in transactions between students and teachers.   From physicist Peter Kazaks, to attend to the interplay between academics and the whole person.  From chemist Paul Scudder, attention to how students misunderstand.  And from sociologist Chick Perrow, the art of teaching by promoting what students do right rather than trying to eliminate what they do wrong.

I have also learned a lot about teaching from some unusual opportunities over the last decade. At the University of Southern California I was affiliated with the Iovine Young Academy for Arts, Technology, and the Business of Innovation.  My colleagues were artists, musicians, engineers, and business professors and our teaching practices evolved as we taught together. Substantively, I revisited my computer science roots, immersed myself in design, and began to rethink innovation and design thinking rooted in social science. At the University of Toronto I was able to split my teaching between courses on computational reasoning - lessons in critical thinking from computer science - and human centered design.  During COVID, like so many others, I developed a new respect for the potential of digitally mediated pedagogy as well as boosting my skills in video production, animation, writing for audio, and rethinking course structures.  Lastly, a stint as Academic Director at the Minerva Project involved a deep dive into active learning practices based in the science of learning and applications of generative AI to teaching and learning. 
##### HOW
I have always been an early adopter, bringing practices into my teaching before they were a thing: agent modeling and emergence in social science courses, using wikipedia editing in instruction, teaching liberal arts students how to code, bending tools for use in digital humanities, creating video course trailers and instructional videos, experimenting with gen AI for curriculum development.

My teaching style tends toward the innovative and experimental, visual and analogical, 
active and experiential.  My classes often involve physical participative exercises to explore concepts: students moving around the room to demonstrate emergent effects in multi-agent systems or game theoretic interactions, acting out data structures, or connecting ourselves together to simulate a neural network.

I am not a fan of learning styles or background essentialism, but believe that students bring to the classroom a variety of kinds and levels of readiness to learn. By offering connections across contexts and disciplines, broad analogies and metaphors, and constant intellectual empathy we can engage with students where they are and guide and support them on the way to where they want to go.

I have long been an advocate of a more sensible and rigorous approach to learning outcomes than the mostly weak version that has come to dominate American higher education.  This was complemented by my experience at Minerva where we thought about courses, course sequences, and whole programs in terms of a learning taxonomy - an explicit articulation of what students should understand and be able to do, the hierarchical relations among these, and the connections between these and methods of assessment.  Done right - as they rarely are - these things offer great potential for increasing the impact of what we do.

Finally, I am firmly committed to the liberal arts as an approach and mindset in education, equally relevant in the small college and an engineering program.  In addition to the natural and social sciences, my own education included work in linguistics, classics, languages, and literature all of which have proved invaluable in both my intellectual and “real world” work.  I bring that sensibility to my pedagogy and believe that its importance only grows with time.
##### THE BIG PICTURE
The motto of my undergraduate institution was "In the final analysis, each student is responsible for their own education."  I think that's right and I think that it increases rather than decreases the responsibility of teachers because we create the environments and contexts where education happens.  As educators in 2024 we have a moral responsibility to craft a curriculum and build institutions in which education turns out to be what's needed for life in the middle third of the 21st century rather than just for the job market of 2025.
