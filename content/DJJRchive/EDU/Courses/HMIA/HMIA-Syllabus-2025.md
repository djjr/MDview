## Course Description

The challenge of ensuring that the actions of individuals and systems — whether human or machine — are consistent with shared goals, reflect our values, and promote societal well-being is known as 'the alignment problem.' Over millennia, humans have developed many coordination and cooperation 'technologies' — such as customs, values, norms, laws, organizations, governments, and markets—that partially solve the problem of human intelligence alignment.As we develop and deploy advanced technologies like artificial intelligence, we are similarly concerned that their use is consistent with shared goals, reflect our values, and promote societal well-being. In this course we will explore the parallels between human intelligence alignment and machine intelligence alignment to help engineers and technologists become reflective practitioners who can grapple wisely with the alignment problem broadly understood. ## Course Purpose and Teaching/Learning Style

Although it meets the FA5 "ethical reflection" foundational abilities requirement, this is not a "computer ethics" or "AI ethics" course in the sense of exploring what are the right and wrong ways of using computers and AI. Those courses typically introduce several schools of thought in ethics and then teach us how to use these to think critically about ethical issues that arise in connection with technology. Implicit in that approach is the idea that if you can teach practitioners to reason ethically about the tools they build and use, then we get technologies and technology uses that are ethical. 

This course shifts to a different level of abstraction by centering on the concept of "alignment" \- ensuring that intelligence behaves in ways that are humanly beneficial. Ethics, building the values into the agents, then becomes just one method among many of aligning intelligence agents (such as us). We will argue that (1) human civilizations have been struggling with intelligence alignment for a long time and (2) that we can become stronger participants in the very important contemporary conversation about AI alignment if we spend some serious time thinking analogically about the ways that humans have come up with for solving intelligence alignment problems in other arenas.

This could be a straight lecture course or a Socratic method course, but I think it will work better as a semi-seminar. That is to say, we will endeavor to be discussion heavy. But a discussion involving 30 people means we don't hear most of the voices in the room, so we will often [break up into smaller groups](https://innoeduvation.com/2019/03/17/pitch-catch-maximizing-peer-critique-and-feedback/) so that everyone gets a little more floor time and a little more peer interaction. Sometimes we will reconvene and "report out" from small groups and sometimes we will expect you to do some reflection on the class discussion in post-class homework.

Which brings me to Pre- and Post-Class Work Assignments. For most of our 27 class sessions, there will be a  **preclass work** assignment. These will usually be in the form of Canvas LMS quiz that include the readings, several "**STOP\+THINK**" questions about each reading, "flashcards" to review important concepts, and, sometimes, other provocations to prepare for class discussion. 

**STOP\+THINK** questions look like this. Your responses to these questions are for your benefit. Suggested responses are available after you type a response, but these are neither graded nor recorded. Sometimes, when sharing responses with classmates is pedagogically called for, PreClass work will make use of Canvas LMS discussion boards instead of **STOP\+THINK** questions. PreClass Work quizzes include one or more "DONE" confirmations. The "grade" for the PreClass Work is the % of these that you mark as done. Honor system. Total points available for PreClassWork is 25\.

Many classes will also have a PostClassWork assignment. PostClassWork is also usually a Canvas LMS quiz that ask you to reflect or build on the class session's material. These are also worth up to one point each depending on what fraction of the tasks are marked "DONE" by the deadline.

There are no extensions for PreClass and PostClass Work assignments Their value lies in their completion between classes.

## Course Objectives

Upon successful completion of this course students should be able to 

* Cogently analyze alignment problem scenarios across human, organizational, expert, and machine intelligence contexts using the context, perspectives, principles, consequences, and course of action framework .  
* Identify and explain a range of alignment problems and alignment solutions, reasoning analogically across human, organizational, expert, and machine intelligence contexts.  
* Be able to speak competently to the point "Intelligence alignment is not a new problem" for seven minutes.  
* Read a research paper and extract useful insights even if not able to understand all of the paper's technical details.

# Schedule

## 08.26 | Welcome and Overview

Stuart Russell says "alignment is the most important question of your life" and Dan Ryan says "this is not our first rodeo." We meet the concept of AI alignment and say a few words about "the four alignment problems" as we introduce the raison d'etre and planned itinerary of the course. PostClass we try out our Alignment Guide GPT.

## 08.28 | AI Bootcamp 0

We assume no prior familiarity with AI and we will not become experts overnight.  But let's get the lay of the land and a foundational understanding of the main concepts.  In class, we'll turn ourselves into a human neural network to illustrate how these things work. PostClass we train and share a [Teachable Machine Model.](https://teachablemachine.withgoogle.com/)

## 09.02 | Alignment 101

We also assume no prior familiarity with AI alignment and we will not become alignment or safety experts in this course. But, again, we can get the lay of the land and start building up our vocabulary. PostClass we get set up on GitHub for how we will share our work during the semester.

## 09.04 | How to Read Hard Things

Reading hard papers is hard, especially when we venture beyond our home intellectual territory.  We will absorb the wisdom of two teachers who have thought about how to teach this skill, S Keshav of the University of Waterloo and V, Bengston of the University of Southern California. Then we will apply these methods to a paper that will be central to the rest of the course. [Almodei et al. 2016 Concrete Problems in AI Safety](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1606.06565).  PostClass we extract some core ideas from the paper and create our first set of "alignment cards."

### This is not our first alignment rodeo

## 09.09 | Four Alignment Moments

Although it is the problem of the moment, the alignment of machine intelligence with human welfare is not the first alignment challenge humans have faced. Today we map out an alignment landscape with four compass points: human agents aligning with one another, the alignment of organizational intelligence, the alignment of magical intelligence, and the alignment of machine intelligence.  PostClass, we invoke Lumen for an example scenario from each compass point.           

## 09.11 | Analogical Thinking: Alignment Problems, Solutions, Cards

PreClass we learn about analogical thinking and a curious question. Having established the four alignment arenas, we introduce the skill of analogical thinking and use it to find analogs of Amodei et al.'s five concrete problems in the alignment of human, organizational, and expert intelligences.  To break the ice we explore the analogy between scalable oversight and a song from the musical "Fiddler on the Roof." Then we spend some time generating structured analogies that relate our five concrete problems and our four alignment moments.

## 09.16 | Aligning Human Level Intelligence: The Problem of Social Order

NOTE: ([DSAI AI in Daily Life Symposium](https://ai.jhu.edu/event/fall-2025-symposium/) is today)

Human social order is an achievement. What does that mean? How do social scientists understand the achievement of human intelligence alignment?

## 09.18 | Organizing Intelligence: Principals and Agents

Bureaucracy is the original artificial intelligence. WHAT?\! Building organizations allows humans to amplify the effects of their intelligence but it comes at a cost.

## 09.23 | Living with Superior Intelligence: Aligning Magicians, Experts, and Professionals

What do Mesopotamian magicians, Roman soothsayers, and medieval conjurers have to do with the professional ethics of lawyers, doctors, and engineers?  Every society, it turns out, deals with the problem of balancing the need for some superintelligences with keeping them aligned with either the public interest or the interests of rulers and elites. 

## 09.25 | Isn't It Just "Ethics"?

This module is a very lightweight Ethics 101 primer.  We'll read Wikipedia articles on Ethics; Professional Ethics; AI Ethics. Our goal is to get familiar with some conceptual vocabulary.  In class we will work through a pragmatic framework for grappling with ethical situations: we establish the context and describe the ethical issues; we assess the perspectives of different stakeholders; we realistically investigate impacts and consequences; we identify the relevant principles and describe how they support the course of action that we recommend. To practice this we work with our EthicsBot and create and analyze different scenarios and then we'll write an essay for formative feedback.

## 09.30 | Why Principles Are Not Enough

The default in conversations about ethical or safe AI is "teach the machines to have human values\!" This is how we try to create ethical doctors, lawyers, and engineers, after all.  This class explores whether this is enough.

## 10.02 Spare

### Align is a Verb

## 10.07 | How Humans Do It: Shared Meaning

One explanation for how humans manage alignment with one another and their communities is that they "share meaning" \- that my ideas are not all MY ideas but rather they match and mirror the ideas of people around me.  This is the basic idea that we can coordinate because we have "shared values."  The question this raises for social science is where does shared meaning come from?  We'll start by reading a short selection from Karl Marx called "The Production of Consciousness." 

## 10.09 | How Humans Do It: Hierarchy

The idea that social order can only be achieved by putting someone in charge is attributed to Thomas Hobbes.  We (re)read a selection from Leviathan and then three synthetic excerpts from Engels' The Origin of the Family, Private Property, and the State.  We contrast Hobbes' suggestion that hierarchy is an alignment solution to social complexity and Engels' idea that it was a response to the breakdown of previously functional alignment mechanisms. 

## 10.14 | How Humans Do It: Groups

Groups and norms and decentralized enforcement is the secret magic sauce of human civilization. Keep in mind the question of whether we could build machines that can do this.

## 10.21 | How Humans Do It: Markets

We read Hayek's "Cosmos and Taxis" as an introduction to the emergence of spontaneous order. A second reading is from Thomas Schelling (on spontaneous emergence of non-alignment) and a selection of quotes from Adam Smith round out the set.

## 10.23 | Updating Our Problem and Solution Taxonomies

At this point we have read about alignment problems among humans, organizations, experts, and machines. We'll begin to forge these ideas into a taxonomy.

## 10.28 | Introduce alignment cards

Our course project will be to produce/curate a deck of "alignment cards" \- each describing an alignment problem or solution and how it manifests in various contexts.

## 10.30 | Agent Traits

What traits should an agent have in order to be aligned or alignable? We'll start by asking how we can ensure the alignment of our classmates and then build from there.

## 11.04 | Qualification

How have humans used qualification and certification to promote alignment? Does it work the same across different contexts?  Would it work for machine intelligences? 

## 11.06 | Transparency and Record Keeping

One of the super powers of bureaucracy is record keeping. Does it have a more general application?

## 11.11 | Control & Oversight

Organizations again: if you want something done right, keep an eye on the person doing it. But that problem of scalable oversight appears again and again.

## 11.13 | Deterrence

Agents can save time by not being careful. Agents can save resources by cutting corners.  Agents can find a shorter path by breaking rules. By maintaining a link between actions and consequences, deterrence reinforces shared standards and discourages future failure — even when internal motivation or oversight is limited.

## 11.18 | Incentive Design

Incentives create a market for alignment.  We endeavor to  create convergence between an agent's private interest and a system's collective goals: the best thing for the agent is the right thing for the system.

## 11.20 | Safety

"Safety" is so obvious that it can be hard to define. But we use it a lot to align intelligences.

## 11.25/7 | No class

## 12.02 | Structures and Institutional Design

Structural and institutional design refers to things that distribute, constrain, and audit power. Rooted in a long tradition of political and institutional design, these mechanisms treat power as natural and inherently fallible and alignment as structurally fragile. Their goal is not to make agents more virtuous or governable or to specify acceptable and unacceptable behaviors, but to make systems of agents alignable even when agents fail.

## 12.04 | Conversations with Our Father: Norbert Wiener and Our Future

Norbert Wiener (1894–1964) invented the field of cybernetics, which he described as “the science of communication as it relates to living things and machines” — but which we now often understand as the science of control.  In this session, we’ll close out our semester by reading and reflecting on his writing about purpose, feedback, error correction, system fragility, and the ethical dangers of optimization without alignment. Wiener is the author of what may be the original alignment warning — a quote as relevant to AI today as it was to missiles in 1947: “If we use, to achieve our purposes, a mechanical agency with whose operation we cannot efficiently interfere… then we had better be quite sure that the purpose put into the machine is the purpose which we really desire and not merely a colorful imitation of it.”
