### Teaching a drone controller by gradient descent
<split even>

![](https://innoeduvation.org/images/images-HMIA/AI101/PID-challenge.excalidraw.svg)

![](https://innoeduvation.org/images/images-HMIA/AI101/PID-drone-loss-landscape01.excalidraw.svg)

</split>

Note:
A lot of technology invention is motivated by problems generated by warfare. This one is not an exception.
Landing a plane on a boat.
PID controller video.
Three knobs. 
STOP+THINK. How would you learn what the right settings are for the kind of flight you would like to steer your drone on?
What happens when I adjust the P knob slightly when the three knobs are in positions $p_i,d_i,i_i$? Does performance get better or worse?
We had one setting P,I,D and that produced a flight that was so so. We try a different setting and we have a sense of the flight being better or worse.  We probably also had some intuition about how the quality of the flight depended on those values. 
But we get frustrated because sometimes it seems that our settings are just in a bad place. We can make small adjustments to get to the best possible but it seems pretty bad compared to what should be possible.
What you just described — adjust one thing, observe the effect, use that to decide your next adjustment — has a name. It's the core idea behind how neural networks learn. Except they have 100 billion knobs, and they do it automatically, millions of times per second.

### LLMs as black boxes: from next token to competent assistant

"Predict" = Word>Embed>Transform>Probability over words>Select
Take the entire internet; break it up into pieces, feed the pieces into a model and have it look at word 1 and try to predict word 2, then words 1,2 try to predict word 3, etc. Accumulate the error(loss). Adjust all the weights based on how much they contributed to getting predictions right and wrong.
