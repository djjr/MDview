# The Case Against Government
## A Survey for Pro-Governance Professionals
##### Description
The history of governments is also the history of those who have sought to shrink, constrain, or dismantle them. Some see the state as a necessary evil, others as an outright obstacle to human flourishing. Some argue for leaner, more efficient governance; others predict or advocate that governance itself will be replaced by markets or technology.

This course provides a **practical engagement** with the major intellectual and ideological critiques of government, from classical liberalism and public choice theory to reactionary, techno-libertarian, and post-liberal alternatives. Students will **map out the major strands of governance skepticism**, examine their historical and contemporary influence, and develop strategies for navigating these critiques as professionals committed to making government effective.

Through case studies, debates, and structured engagements with skeptics' arguments, students will come away with a **sharper understanding of governance constraints, ideological fault lines, and the evolving challenges to public authority** -- not to dismiss these critiques, but to grapple with them seriously as forces that shape real -- world policymaking.

##### Outline
#### **1. The State as a Predator (vs. Protector)**
- Government exists primarily to serve itself, exploit its citizens, or entrench elites rather than act in the public interest.
    - **Mancur Olson** (_The Rise and Decline of Nations_)—state as a "stationary bandit."
    - **Buchanan & Tullock** (Public Choice Theory): governments act in their own self-interest.
    - **James Burnham** (_The Managerial Revolution_): bureaucracy as the real ruling class.
#### **2. The State as an Economic Hindrance**
- Markets allocate resources more efficiently than government ever could.
    - **Adam Smith** (_Wealth of Nations_): invisible hand vs. state intervention.
    - **Friedrich Hayek** (_The Road to Serfdom_): central planning leads to tyranny.
    - **Milton Friedman** (_Capitalism and Freedom_): government intervention distorts incentives.
#### **3. The State as a Tyrant**
- Governments inevitably infringe on liberty, so power must be minimized.
    - **Thomas Paine** (_Common Sense_): government as a necessary evil.
    - **Robert Nozick** (_Anarchy, State, and Utopia_): the minimal state.
    - **Carl Schmitt** (_The Concept of the Political_): liberal states are weak in real crises.
#### **4. The State as an Ineffective Problem-Solver**
- Bureaucracy is structurally incapable of solving complex problems.
    - **Herbert Simon** (_Administrative Behavior_): bounded rationality and decision-making flaws.
    - **Cato Institute & Libertarian Governance Critiques**: government failures, not just market failures.
    - **Techno-libertarians** (e.g., Nick Land, Curtis Yarvin): state incompetence vs. market/tech-driven solutions.
#### **5. The State as an Obsolete or Temporary Institution**
- Governance itself might be an outdated model, soon to be replaced by alternative systems.
    - **Marx & Engels** (_The Communist Manifesto_): the state will wither away.
    - **Nick Bostrom & AI Governance Theorists**: technological decision-making will surpass human governance.
    - **Seasteading, Crypto-Governance**: experimental governance models outside state control.
