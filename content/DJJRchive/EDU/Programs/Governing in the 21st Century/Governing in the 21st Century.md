# Governing in the 21st Century
I've taught in a public policy program, law schools, an information school,  a program that hybridized engineering, business, and design, and at a plain, old liberal arts college. Although each of these trained students for explicitly different locations in the professional division of labor, they all aspired to do it in a way that prepared them to be effective in the world in which they would pursue their careers and raise their children.

Roughly speaking, that translates to giving people the skills they will need to make a difference over the next 25 years. A pretty straightforward mission.

Our success was hit or miss because only exceptionally did we build curricula based on the world we could see emerging.  We would make ritual bows to "innovation" and "real world experience," but mostly we built programs using yesterday as the blueprint for tomorrow.  Accreditation, received wisdom, what seemed to have worked for us, the need to be more or less like other programs, our own career trajectories, distraction and laziness -- all factors, for sure.

Often, it didn't matter too much. Gravity is gravity; Shakespeare is Shakespeare; a lot of tomorrow's coding work is just like yesterday's coding work. And even things that are evolving usually change slowly enough that a given curriculum can continue to serve more or less well for a generation or more. And when significant discontinuities do appear, our smarter students adapt, not necessarily any thanks to us, so that the old curricular regime seems to have more staying power than it actually has.

There are a lot of interests and incentives supporting the status quo. Change is risky, takes effort, and inevitably upsets important apple carts. But sometimes we reach a point where we realize that the kinds of professionals we are training might be contributing more to the problems than the solutions of our era. 

The challenge is two-sided. Figuring out what the new thing should look like is hard.  Maybe harder is we educators rarely agree on anything more than incremental change and almost never entertain change that threatens the status processes that got us where we are.

### Finding an Angle of Attack
Institutional transformation (and creating new ones is transforming the same-ole-same-ole that would happen without really deliberate efforts to do something else) is so hard that there's single best approach.  All the gurus want to sell you a method and real leaders need mantras and touchstones to stave off chaos-dread, but we might be able to reason our way to some plausible "algorithms."
###### Product First
Start with what kind of output do we need to be producing? What is our vision of the 21st century governance professional? When we look at the fit between our current policy-government graduates and the positions they end up in, what skills and sensibilities do we wish they had?
###### End-user First
What does the world need? What kind of professional skill sets will the places that hire policy-government graduates be looking for in the coming decade?  What should they be looking for? What kinds of roles and organizations that don't even exist do we think will or or should emerge?
###### Start with what does the world that's coming online look like?
Ideate about the world that's just around the corner.  If our job is to design a curriculum that will equip graduates to be effective in the world to come, let's try to get concrete about the features we think that world will have.

For example:
- the world is changing rapidly, perhaps more rapidly than in the past
- problems (increasingly) don't respect disciplinary boundaries
- tech (increasingly) high impact and (increasingly?) highly opaque to experts in other fields
- governance takes place in an environment that contains anti-governance
- fast & total data about everything
- division of decision-making labor with machines
###### Map the Status Quo
What do generic policy/government curricula look like?  Just make sure we share a consensus of the status quo.
**Exercise:** make everyone write up concrete statements about why we don't want to create just another one of these.
###### Reverse Engineer Career Tracks (status quo and projections)
Identify ten career tracks. For each, identify most important/core skills. Apply factor analysis or principal component analysis style thinking to identify the highest impact dimensions of the skill taxonomy implied.
###### Brainstorm Value Proposition Type Taglines
How might one summarize what various types of professional schools train people to do? E.g.:
- "make government work better"
- "manage global interdependence"
- "translate politics and science into policy"
- "translate policy into implementation"
###### Ideate High Level Learning Taxonomy
Think about [[High Level Learning Outcomes]] without thinking about formal things like type of degree, courses, tracks, programs and so on. What are the categories of knowledge and skills a 21st century governance professional ought to possess?
###### Get Explicit about Interdisciplinarity
Government and policy degrees are inherently multidisciplinary, though not surprisingly perhaps dominated by economics, politics, and organizations.  Each discipline is taught in a manner that's less siloed than back in the home departments, but we mostly expect the INTERdisciplinarity to happen in the student.

So, let's get explicit: what do we want to see in our students that would come under the heading "interdisciplinary"?  
##### Not Just Our Students/Graduates
**We** should also aspire to a new kind of interdisciplinarity. Heretofore interdisciplinarity, on average, amounts to team teaching where I take one week and you take the other week.  Or it's Noah's ark: we pat ourselves on the back because we had both sociologists and psychologists at the table.

A larger contributor to multi- rather than inter- disciplinarity is that we faculty have identities, audiences, assessments, turfs, comfort zones, teaching repertoires, and reference groups that are all discipline-based. When we hire prestigious faculty, we hire prestigious economists or political scientists or computer scientists. (one is reminded of the important article "On the folly of rewarding A, while hoping for B")

To create a different kind of school we probably need to create a different kind of interdisciplinarity.  And I think it comes in two, perhaps not orthogonal dimensions. One is personal/intellectual and the other is structural/performative.

On the personal front, it's about finding people who are, or can become, naturally curious and driven to understand and respect other fields.
Many years ago I attended a sociology seminar at Berkeley. The speaker was George Ackerlof, Nobel prize winner in economics, and he was talking about taking sitting in on sociologist Arlie Hochschild's class so he could learn to think more sociologically about certain economic problems. The seminar was about problems he wanted to solve but recognized economics did not provide the tools he would need.

Recently I've been reading neuroscientist Eric Kandel's work.  From an initial in psychoanalysis, his early career was studying the brain physiology of learning. He was soon asking questions his concepts and techniques were not able to answer - when it comes to neurons talking to one another, the action is in proteins and molecules. Fortunately, a new field, molecular biochemistry, was emerging and so he added it to his tool kit.  And then the questions moved in the direction of where the proteins were coming from and so he had to take on the study of genetics. But he never lost interest in the question of the biological basis of mental illness and the way that's studied now is big N population genomic analysis and so that's now a part of his tool box.
###### New Personal Interdisciplinarity
So, the personal side of the new interdisciplinarity is what happens when thinkers genuinely cross train in multiple disciplines. They are problem driven and problems don't care about disciplines.

This kind of thinker is able to "think more" but that's only half the story. They also make for a new kind of colleague and collaborator.  If Noah's ark style interdisciplinary collaboration yields something less than the sum of its parts, collaboration between, say, the political scientist who has learned some computer science and the computer scientist who has learned some politics can be the product of the parts.

###### New Structural Interdisciplinarity
We will need to do more than hope we can find the right people. The place has to be structured in a manner that supports, encourages, and supports new ways to be interdisciplinary.

We might, for example, eschew the idea of organizing the institution as a set of labs centered on each senior scholar and instead, from the outset, build labs around problem areas.  Keep the problems central and the people fungible.

We might, for example, structure reward and assessment in ways that are explicitly cross disciplinary (avoiding for example the bind of asking for reviews of an interdisciplinary scholar from scholars in the two or three disciplines they represent and then finding them wanting because they are not outstanding in any one of the disciplines).
#### Design Strategies
The ideas above represent necessary spade work. Before embarking on design we need this kind of divergent thinking to widen the database on which each of us draws and to deepen the overlaps among the databases on which each of us draws.
##### Insights
Insights are new formulations about the way the world is that help to inform design decisions.  They are what our data says to us.

So we'll need to deliberately search for insights in what we have learned.  What might they look like?

A succinct statement of just what the "standard model" that we don't want to repeat looks like.

An informed statement of what it is that this institution is positioned to do that nobody else is.
#### Do "Is it real? Can we win? Is it worth doing?" Analysis

This is for later in the process.  This draws from an excellent 2007 HRB article of that title.

**Is it real?**
Is the product real? Does the technology to build this thing actually exist? Is this the kind of thing that one can do in one or two years of education? Is it coherent? Legible?
Is the market real? Are there people who would want this training? Are there people who'd hire them if they got it? 
**Can we win?**
Can we win? Is this thing we want to offer really better than the alternatives? How established are the alternatives?  Why would people switch to us?
Can the organization win?  Do we have the human capital and other resources to do this well at the necessary scale and over the necessary time frame?
**Is it worth doing?**
Will this move produce the ROI we need?  Does it make sense as a choice?Does this project make strategic sense? Is it consonant with the path we claim to be on?
Will this effort generate a platform on which other things can be built?
### Provocation as a Design Strategy
With sufficient "spade work" it can be helpful to provoke folks into sketching out fantasy proposals.  If you can get folks this far, it's really useful because these can become the basis for really substantive conversations. Imagine, for example, someone throws out the idea for a course structured around alternating weeks of work and study. Sounds cool - but now let's dissect the idea. What are the virtues? What does it afford? What else could you do if this were a part of the institution? And ask the person who dreamed it up to defend it, explain what they think it could accomplish, etc.

In that spirit, I sketched out the following (incomplete curriculum).

### Modest Proposal for a Curriculum
What if there were a school designed not to replicate the past but to train the government professionals the mid-21st century needs? A schooln the CC big Enron🍙🌮 that deliberately resists the temptation to measure its success by how closely it resembles 20th-century legacy programs—no matter how excellent they were. Instead of defaulting to possibly outdated assumptions, this program would make a deliberate bet on the mindsets and skills that will enable governments to secure abundance in a world transformed by AI, climate change, and geopolitical upheaval.

The curriculum would be designed with ruthless intentionality: retaining a carefully curated selection of what works while discarding elements that serve disciplinary research interests rather than student training needs. Above all, it would be forward-looking—a strategic blueprint for governance in an era where the accelerating deployment of AI, the physical and economic consequences of climate change, the restructuring of global power, and the fracturing of once-stable institutions are reshaping what effective government must be.
## Courses
### Context
###### [[A Short History of Reinventing Government]]
 - the "least you must know" about the historical forces that are recorded in contemporary challenges of governance.
###### Civics 101: anatomy of governance in US
 - what every government professional needs to know about how the damn thing works (and is supposed to work)
###### [[The Case Against Government]]
 - examines major intellectual critiques of governance, from classical liberalism and public choice theory to reactionary and techno-libertarian thought. Students will grapple with the strongest arguments against government to better understand challenges shaping public policy today.
###### Policy and Governance as Response to Market and Alignment Failure
 - expands the conventional idea of policy/regulation as response to market failure to alignment failure and world of intelligent agents
### Toolbox 1

###### [[Maxims and Mantras - Pros and Cons of the Pithy Axioms of Public and Private Leadership]] 
 - organizations, innovation, and governance explored through a serious and critical examination of buzzy turns of phrase
###### [[Uncertainty for Practitioners]]
 - Although this is our "intro to stats" course, it is structured around training the practitioner to have the reflex see every observation or experience is a sample from a distribution. Our exploration of probability, decision trees, risk, and prediction are built on top of this.
###### [[Systems Thinking]]
 - explores networks of cause and effect that shape complex governance challenges, from environmental policy to digital regulation. Students learn to map these dynamics, generate data-driven (including AI/ML) insights, and design interventions that yield resilience, adaptability, and sustainable outcomes.
###### [[Service Design for Government]]
 - equips government professionals with the tools to think like service designers—without becoming designers.
###### [[Finding Out - Research Methods for the Practical Policy Activist]]
 - how to carry out, understand, and assess a variety of research methods. Focus on developing judgment about method selection and trade-offs, understanding the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches, and cultivating respect for the role of method in producing reliable insights.
###### Introduction to Computational Reasoning
 - gentle introduction to the concepts and styles of thinking of computer science for literacy, collaborative competence, and further study. 
###### [[Analytical Tools for Critical Policy-Making and Implementation]]
 - modeling and simulation for policy
###### [[Problem Solving Five Ways]]
 - introduces five distinct professional approaches - rational, creative, engineering, legal, and political - teaching students when and how to apply each. By the end, they will have a flexible problem-solving toolkit and the ability to collaborate across disciplines.
### Toolbox 2: Nitty Gritty
###### The least you Should Know: what, whence, why, and whither
- Repertory course offered every term with varying content.  All students are expected to have competent familiarity with a wide range of policy/governance areas. Each offering of this class includes multiple crash-courses in substantive areas like poverty, transportation, climate, AI, cybersecurity, healthcare, housing, crime and policing, economic development.
###### Procurement for Quality and Efficient Problem Solving
 - Contracts, staying in the drivers seat, trade shows, learning from vendors, compliance, negotiating, involving end users
###### [[Building Regulatory Infrastructure to Secure Abundance]] 
 - How effective regulation enables markets, innovation, and economic prosperity. 
###### Social/Legal/Political Dynamics of Information
 - What analytical and implementation tools does the modern policy implementer need if information is the new oil?
## The \[OurSchool] Way
These courses are core elements of the curricular "brand." They are the gospel and the "where the magic happens" part of the curriculum.

###### Driving Quality and Value in Public Investment
 - This class introduces students to a mindset and philosophy of governance that informs the entire curriculum.  It is built around an affirmative belief that there are skills and practices and structures and institutions that enable governments to produce good outcomes for humans.  The class that teaches students how we can "have nice things." Drawing on international comparison cases we build our toolkit for how governments can produce value for citizens.
###### Translational Intervention and Implementation I, II, III
- Multidisciplinary experiential/lab course in which we model and teach the art of doing the possible and the maybe-not-possible. First semester is skills, background, and labs. Second semester supervised local projects. Third semester independent team projects.
