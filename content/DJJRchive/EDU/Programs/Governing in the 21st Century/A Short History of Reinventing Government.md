# A Short History of Reinventing Government
##### Description
How did the world get become the one in which your professional career will unfold? This course distills a kind of "least you must know" about the historical forces that are recorded in contemporary governance. Rather than a comprehensive survey, we take an opportunistic, thematic approach, focusing on key moments when societies rethought governance - whether in response to crisis, revolution, war, or technological upheaval - and laid down patterns we continue to live with.

Alongside the historical narrative, we examine the intellectual foundations that emerged in each era - how political philosophy, economic thought, and bureaucratic models evolved in response to governance challenges. By tracing these cycles of governance failure, adaptation, and innovation, students will develop a historically informed strategic mindset for navigating contemporary governance challenges.

##### Outline
#### **1. Crisis and Constitution: Designing Durable Governance**
Governance Moment: building a functioning republic after revolution.
- **England after the Civil War (1640s-1689)**: The balance of power, constitutional limits on monarchy.
    - Hobbes (_Leviathan_, 1651) and Locke (_Two Treatises of Government_, 1689) - debates on state authority, social contract, and governance by consent.
- **American Revolution & U.S. Constitution (1776–1789)**:  How to prevent tyranny while avoiding anarchy.
    - The Federalist Papers (selected essays: Nos. 10 (faction/polarization), 51 (separation of powers, governance as system), 70 (executive action)).
- **France Post-Revolution (1789–1799)**: Why revolutionary ideals failed to produce a stable government; birth of bureaucracy and state rationalization.
    - Sieyès ([_What is the Third Estate?_](https://revolution.chnm.org/d/280)), Tocqueville (_The Old Regime and the Revolution_). Rousseau (_The Social Contract_, 1762) - general will and direct democracy vs. administrative centralization.
- **Europe after 1848**: Revolutionary failures and conservative adaptations.
    - Marx and Engels (_Communist Manifesto_, 1848) - state as an instrument of class, early ideas of governance for redistribution.
#### **2. Building the Modern State: Rationalizing Governance**
- **Unification of Germany & Italy (1860s-1870s)**: Bureaucracy as state-building, realpolitik.
    - Hegel’s ideas on the rational state (_Philosophy of Right_, 1820) and nationalism’s role in state legitimacy.
- **Progressive Era in the US (1890s-1920s)**: Regulation, science-driven policy, emergence of expertise.
    - Pragmatism—Dewey on democracy and expertise, Weber (_Politics as a Vocation_, 1919) on the bureaucratic state.
- **World War I & Political Economy (1914-1918)**: Centralized planning, war economies.
    - Veblen (_The Engineers and the Price System_, 1921) - technocracy as a response to market inefficiencies.
#### **3. The State and the Social Contract: The Rise of Public Policy**
- **Post-WWII Welfare State Expansion (1945-1960s)**: State as economic stabilizer and provider.
    - Keynesianism - Keynes (_General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money_, 1936); Rawls (_A Theory of Justice_, 1971) - justice as fairness, redistribution as social contract.
- **Cold War Governance (1945-1989)**: Policy by game theory, strategic rationality.
    - RAND Corporation & Nash Equilibrium in political science; Berlin (_Two Concepts of Liberty_, 1958) - negative vs. positive liberty in governance.
- **Neoliberalism & Deregulation (1970s-1990s)**: Markets as governance mechanism.
    - Hayek (_The Road to Serfdom_, 1944); Buchanan & Public Choice Theory (government as a self-interested actor).
#### **4. The Technological Age and Reinventing Government**
- **Digital Governance (1990s-present)**: Data-driven policymaking. 
	- Gore et al. "The National Partnership for Reinventing Government (NPR)"
	- [Reinventing Government -- Two Decades Later](https://www.govexec.com/management/2013/04/what-reinvention-wrought/62836/)
	- [Reinventing government: Reflections 30 years later](https://www.govexec.com/management/2023/09/reinventing-government-reflections-30-years-later/390046/)
    - Foucault (_Discipline and Punish_, 1975) - state as a system of control and surveillance; Beniger (_The Control Revolution_, 1986) on bureaucratic information processing.
- **AI and the Future of Bureaucracy**: Predictive governance, algorithmic decision-making.
    - Wiener (_Cybernetics_, 1948) on automated governance; contemporary concerns on machine ethics (e.g., Bostrom on AI governance).
    - LePore (_If Then: How the Simulmatics Corporation Invented the Future_)

Bending points and breaking points.  Governance contests vs governance voids (to fill). Delegitimization as phase change?

The phrase "recorded in contemporary governance" is a machine learning metaphor. The idea is that governance institutions are decision-making systems that map situations as input to organizational actions as outputs. See [[Institutions as Functions and Agents]].
