# On Meetings
###### Dan Ryan
How many meetings do you have in a week? How awesome are they? How often do you feel like a meeting significantly moved the ball down the field?

In an average N person meeting, assuming no cross talk, most participants are idly listening most of the time.  The amount starts at 50% for a two person meeting and then approaches 100% as meetings grow larger:$$\text{idle time} = \frac{N-1}{N} \times \text{meeting length}$$ Of course, it's not quite accurate to say non-speakers are "idle" - most of are thinking about what they will say and angling to get the floor.

In this context our evaluation of meeting quality often involves things like "did everyone get a chance to speak?" "did we get through the agenda?" "did we resolve anything?"  

The last of these expresses the implicit purpose of most meetings - to draw on the experience, expertise, and interests of a set of folks to inform a decision or develop a set of options.  Some prior organizational work has brought us to a fork in the road and we think we need a few more eyes on the it to decide which path to take.  Or, prior organizational work has gotten a task to a certain point and we need to generate more raw material to be used in moving forward.

Those things describe having a meeting with a purpose and goal in mind. But in practice, I think most meetings are held because 
- we have no idea what to do
- we get nervous about working in isolation (getting out of sync with collaborators)
- we want to (gently sometimes) remind people of the hierarchy they are a part of
- we held one the same time last week (month, year)

On top of these things is the two top-most observation I've made about meetings over the years:
- people who call and run meetings rarely prepare adequately for them
- people who call meetings never reckon with the organizational cost of a meeting
	- simple person hours $(\text{size of meeting} \times \text{length of meeting})$ 
	- implicit message broadcast about how organized and competent leadership is

#### How to Do Better?
- Treat meeting planning and preparation as a serious task
- Force a reckoning of meeting cost and expected meeting benefit
- Invite the people who are needed to contribute to the goal of the meeting
- Be smart about synchronous and asynchronous activity

Choreographing the synchronous and asynchronous often feels too heavy handed, but it can mean everyone's time is better spent.  Compare a meeting in which the chair says "I wonder what people think about idea X..." and then calls on someone to start with a plan to allow the conversation to continue for 5 minutes to a meeting in which the chair says "how about everyone take 2 minutes to jot down what they think about idea X..." and then we'll quickly go round and hear from folks in turn, clockwise.

In the first scenario, someone starts speaking before they've had a chance to think. Others don't listen as they scramble to compose what they will say if called on. First person goes on and on because still formulating. Others adjust what they would say based on perceived reactions to first speaker. In the second scenario, we plumb each expert in the room independently, we give them a moment to compose their thinking, we get them to commit to a set of words, going round the room generates an increment of common knowledge about the dispersed knowledge in reaction to idea X.

## Meeting tips from tech guys (h/t [SkillPacks](https://www.skillpacks.com/elon-musk-meetings/))

Walk out of a meeting or drop off a call as soon as it is obvious you aren’t adding value. - Elon Musk
Communication should travel via the shortest path necessary to get the job done, not through the “chain of command”. - Elon Musk
There are only a few good reasons for having meetings: generating ideas and solving problems are two of the best. And to solve problems, the meeting requires a clear statement of the problem, and the right structure and facilitation to keep focused on problem-solving. - Elon Musk
Focus on Outcomes: Prioritize actionable insights and decisions over lengthy discussions. - Satya Nadella.
Silent Start: Begin meetings by silently reading the prepared memos, fostering in-depth understanding. -Jeff Bezos
High Standards: Maintain high standards for preparation and participation to ensure productive discussions. -Jeff Bezos
Set Clear Agendas: Define the purpose and desired outcomes to keep discussions focused. - Google
Time Management: Keep meetings short and concise to maintain energy and focus.  - Google
