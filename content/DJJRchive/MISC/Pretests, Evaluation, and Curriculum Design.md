In the precincts of teaching and training, we love pre-tests, surveys of prior experience, student evaluations of teaching or learning, summative assessments and all that stuff.
We seem to believe that you get more learning when you measure it or that WE are such learning-oriented teachers that we will adjust our methods based on what works and what doesn't.
Maybe so.
But what if we used surveys, tests, and evaluations to plan our teaching? Sounds like a chicken and egg thing?  Yes. And so I mean use these as a thinking framework for thinking about how and what we teach.
Suppose we have an assessment question such as "Briefly explain the difference between organic and inorganic chemistry."  We expect the average lay person or beginning student to say "I think organic chemistry means the chemistry of things that are or were alive and inorganic is the chemistry of minerals and non-living stuff."  It's not a wrong answer, but it's "untutored."  We can imagine a student at the end of a year of chemistry study to respond with something like "Organic chemistry focuses on compounds that contain carbon, especially those with carbon-hydrogen bonds, which form the basis of most biological molecules. Inorganic chemistry deals with compounds that generally don’t have carbon-hydrogen bonds, including metals, minerals, and salts."
In a workshop on AI, we might hope to see a transformation like this:
![](https://innoeduvation.org/images/AI-explainers/silhouette_learningAboutAI.png)
In other cases we might think more in terms of shifting the distribution. We have some assessment criteria and we expect the initial distribution to be skewed way to the left - almost no one knows anything about the topic at hand.  What's our goal?  A different distribution.  Perhaps for the first lesson we'd be happy to get to something resembling a normal distribution.
![](https://innoeduvation.org/images/AI-explainers/from-low-knowledge-to-some-knowledge.png)
In both cases we can think critically about two things: does the right side articulate what we are trying to accomplish? And, what is the arrow? In other words, what are we doing in the teaching that is supposed to get us from the left state to the right state.
There is also a third benefit from this framing.  All too often we pre-survey or post-test mostly because that's what people do and because we feel we are fumbling around in the dark and "do a survey" always sounds rational and sophisticated.  Situating real or imagined survey results on two ends of an arrow that represents a transformation we will be accountable for can make for better surveys and tests.
