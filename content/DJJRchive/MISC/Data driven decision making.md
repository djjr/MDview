2025-01-17 In some precincts, "data driven decision making" (DDDM) has grandma and apple pie status.  What does it mean?  Usually, the contrast is with intuition-based decision making or some variation of interest-based decision making.  

The basic idea is that if a goal X is selected by some process, then the means of achieving X should be selected based on the best available knowledge about how to bring X about.  It might also be that we want to achieve X without causing Y, and the same thing applies. We might also want to inform the selection of goal X with data: if we choose to achieve X, what other things will happen or not happen? Again, data driven decision making (this time about the goal), might help.

Some of the enthusiasm for DDDM comes from those who are in the data business, especially schools and the professionals they credential.  Some comes from political actors who believe "the data is on our side."

Don't get me wrong, I'm a fan.  But the real world eats good ideas like DDDM for breakfast.  On the way to becoming institutionalized, we can expect to see politicization of data collection and retention as a way for special interests to manipulate outcomes "legitimately" and the elevation of output and process over outcome. In other words, we focus on data, data analysis, and being "driven" by the data and lose sight of the outcome we wanted: higher quality decisions.  

Keeping a firm eye on [[Outcomes, not outputs]] is always a good idea.
