"Prototype" is still a sexy word even if design thinking is a little bit 20teens. This is unfortunate because sexy words get used a lot but often don't carry a whole lot of meaning.

In some literal sense a prototype is an early model. It's an early draft. As such it's really not that sexy unless you consider the rather mundane idea that most work is somewhat iterative to be a breakthrough insight.  Admittedly, in some precincts the idea that first attempts can be improved upon is a novel approach, but let's set those aside.

Prototypes as drafts is a great start but there's more work to be done if we want to ground the sexiness of "prototype" because "draft" is not a sexy idea. We can make progress on this if we ask "why make prototypes?" Here's my answer: prototypes allow the world to speak to you about your idea.

Eh?

When we set about making something - a email to a colleague, soup for the kids, a small accounting application, a table for the front hall, a redesign for our kitchen - we start out by thinking. We think about the thing and we think about the world. Tall table or short table. How will it fill the space. Does it end up being for keys and mail or something else? Wood or metal? Thin top or thick top. Will it scare the dog? This thinking devotes a lot of "compute" to simulating the world. I simulate size and shape and shadows and textures. I simulate interactions and perspectives.  All of these are essentially queries that I pose to the world, but instead of asking the world directly, I ask my simulated version of the world.
![](https://innoeduvation.org/images/images-design/prototyping%20simulating%20the%20world.png)
Why not let the world participate in this conversation?

That's what prototyping is about - letting the world have a say.

Every prototype is asking the world a question: how does tall and skinny look in our hallway? how tall and skinny can it be before it's unstable? how wide should it be if I want to throw my keys on it? could something elegant be made out of cardboard? will the family dog tolerate ANY furniture added to the hall?

Intentional prototyping is prototyping with a self-consciousness about what we want to get from the prototype.

We can express this epistemologically, that is, in terms of how we are using a prototype to expand our knowledge.  In general, there are things we know, things we think we know, things we know we don't know, and things we don't know that we don't know. Different kinds of intentional prototyping are appropriate in different situations.

##### Prototyping for What We Know - Communicate the Idea

Sometimes, we need a prototype simply to make an idea tangible. It helps us—and our boss, investor, or team—see that _"this could be a thing!"_ Prototypes also align teams; with just words, everyone holds their own mental model, but seeing a prototype reveals differences: _“Hmm, Jake’s thinking about this very differently than I am.”_ Beyond alignment, prototypes help us **commit** to an idea. Before something tangible exists, concepts remain fluid—_maybe this, maybe that_—which can make critical evaluation difficult. A prototype turns possibilities into something real, anchoring both feedback and decision-making.

##### Prototyping for What We Think We Know - Confirm Our Assumptions

Most creative processes aren’t grounded solely in proven facts—they’re built on assumptions and hypotheses. Recognizing these assumptions is essential, and prototyping offers a practical way to test them. Through prototyping, we can confirm basic assumptions, explore critical hypotheses, or deepen our understanding of core concepts. Sometimes, our work hinges on a key insight or a unique idea that sets it apart from existing solutions. In these cases, a proof-of-concept prototype helps validate that foundational premise and ensures we're not fundamentally off track.

##### Prototyping for What We Know We Don't Know - Answer Our Questions

Recognizing what we don’t know is a crucial skill. When exploring a new idea, we often realize that while the first few steps are clear, the path beyond remains uncertain. Sometimes, we can already see that the journey is filled with “forks in the road,” where critical decisions will shape the outcome. Prototyping helps navigate this uncertainty in two key ways. **Stepwise refinement** allows us to “defer detail,” starting with simple, broad strokes—whether from the top, the outside, or a minimal version—to create something tangible that reveals the next step. **Experimental prototyping** isolates anticipated forks, letting us compare materials, styles, or technologies to see which performs best or is easiest to use.

##### Prototyping for What We Don't Know that We Don't Know - Find the Unexpected

Prototypes are most critical when exploring what we don’t know we don’t know. The world—whether through physics, environmental factors, or users' cultural perspectives—holds knowledge we can't access until we put a prototype into it. No matter how carefully we plan or how many frameworks we follow, surprises about form, functionality, and usability emerge only when an object is in a user's hands. Field testing uncovers unanticipated environmental interactions. Early production reveals fabrication challenges we hadn’t considered. Political, legal, and cultural responses surface through real interactions between people, institutions, and tangible prototypes. Prototyping, in this sense, becomes a dialogue with the unknown, allowing the world to answer questions we didn't know we had.
