It is helpful for teams to develop a "team culture," but it takes time and sometimes doesn't happen because we are in too much of a hurry to get on with the task at hand.  This exercise is a way to jump-start the process.

Team members have a deck of "team norm" cards.  Usually these are about team member behavior vis a vis teammates.  But the same exercise can work for developing shared vision of team mission, style of work, etc.  We start with facilitator talking about the importance of shared values, common approaches to work, a common sense of expectations, the capacity of team members to engage in gentle, good cheer decentralized norm enforcement.

During heads down time, each team member chooses a few cards they would like to nominate to be one of their team's norms.

After timer goes, we go around the table clockwise and each person has, say, 30 seconds, to nominate one of their selected norms. They say what it is, elaborate as needed, explain why it's important to them.  Go around the table 2 or 3 times or until all the suggestions are "on the table."
Then allow people to rate each suggestion. Different ways to do this. One is everyone gets to vote for any idea they deem important and to rate first, second, third priority.  Then we arrange things on two dimensions - how many people said it mattered and how the idea faired in terms of priority. Then we talk about selecting norms for the team. Can tweak by constraining to be one less than the number of team members to prevent "every body gets one" and other things like that.

#### Pedagogical Note
There's a certain amount of curated bootstrapping going on here. In the case of team norms, the cards represent ideas borrowed from lots of different sources on how to run good teams.  So, we are seeding the conversation with known good ideas. But leaving it up to each person to think about and interpret and put their own spin on each one when they share with team.  And team gets to see where they align and where they don't, what different experiences people have had with teams, etc.

We can do a similar thing if we are working with teams who will have a concrete task (e.g., come up with an AI application to help residents of small cities).  Instead of team norms we might have design and creative problem solving provocations (e.g., "start small" "proof of concept first" "stay focused on outcomes not outputs" etc.).
