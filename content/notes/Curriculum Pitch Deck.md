# Governing in the 21st Century

## A master's program designed for the world that's actually coming

*[School Name] — Curriculum Proposal*

---

## The honest diagnosis

> "We would make ritual bows to 'innovation' and 'real world experience,' but mostly we built programs using yesterday as the blueprint for tomorrow."

Policy and governance schools are full of smart, dedicated people — and they are, by and large, producing the wrong professionals for the era we're in.

Not because they're lazy. Because the incentives are stacked against change:
- Accreditation rewards resemblance to existing programs
- Faculty hire and reward disciplinary depth, not interdisciplinary range
- Curricula reflect what faculty know how to teach, not what students will need
- The students who do adapt do so *despite* us, not because of us

**The question isn't whether to change. It's whether we have the nerve to.**

---

## The world that's coming

The governance professional of 2040 works in a world legacy programs were not designed for:
- **AI and automation** are restructuring the division of decision-making labor between humans and machines — including in government
- **Climate change** is producing cascading, nonlinear policy challenges that don't respect disciplinary or jurisdictional boundaries
- **Anti-governance is a movement** — state legitimacy won't be defended by competence alone
- **Inverted information asymmetry** — total data but no institutional capacity to use it well
- **Geopolitical realignment** is redrawing the rules of multilateral cooperation
Will our graduates be equipped to govern tomorrow's world, or yesterday's?

---

## The professional we're training

The 21st century governance professional is not a specialist who occasionally collaborates. They are a **genuine cross-trainer** — problem-driven, not discipline-driven.

Think George Akerlof sitting in on Arlie Hochschild's sociology seminar because economics didn't have the tools he needed. Think Eric Kandel adding molecular biology, then genetics, then population genomics to his toolkit — because the problem kept pulling him forward.

---

## Our graduate:
- Sees every observation as a sample from a distribution
- Can map a system before trying to fix it
- Knows five ways to frame a problem and when each is indicated
- Has genuine comfort with uncertainty, ambiguity, and complexity
- Can work with — not just alongside — technologists, designers, lawyers, and data scientists
- Understands the strongest arguments *against* government well enough to answer them

**This is a different kind of professional. It requires a different kind of program.**

---

## Our curricular philosophy

Three commitments that distinguish this program from every other one:

**1. Ruthless intentionality**

**2. Genuine interdisciplinarity**

**3. Forward-looking by design**

Let us explain each.

---


## **1. Ruthless intentionality**

Every element of the curriculum earns its place by answering one question: *Does this make our graduates more effective in the world that's coming?* Disciplinary traditions, received wisdom, and what got us our own jobs are not sufficient justifications.

---

## **2. Genuine interdisciplinarity**

Not Noah's Ark (one of each). Not team-teaching (I take this week, you take that one). Real cross-training, where faculty and students alike develop fluency across domains because the problems demand it.

---

## **3. Forward-looking by design**

The accelerating deployment of AI, the consequences of climate change, the fracturing of once-stable institutions — these aren't electives. They are the operating environment. The curriculum is built around them, not retrofitted for them.

---

## Curriculum architecture
The program is organized into three integrated layers:

**Context — *Why does the world look like this?***  Historical and intellectual foundations; the forces, ideas, and failures that produced the governance challenges students will spend their careers addressing.

**Toolbox — *How do you actually work on hard problems?***  Analytical, methodological, and practical skills of professional competence: uncertainty, systems thinking, research methods, service design, problem-solving frameworks, regulatory design.

**The OUR Wa — *What does it mean to do this work well?***  Signature courses and sequences that constitute the program's identity: a philosophy of public value, and a three-semester lab where students do the real thing.

*The layers are not sequential. They run concurrently and reinforce each other.*

---

## Context: Knowing where you are

**A Short History of Reinventing Government**
The "least you must know" — from Hobbes and Locke through the Progressive Era, the welfare state, neoliberalism, and the algorithmic present. Not a comprehensive survey; a strategically selective account of the forces recorded in today's governance challenges.

**The Case Against Government**
A course for pro-governance professionals that takes the opposition seriously. Students work through the strongest arguments for shrinking, constraining, or dismantling the state — from public choice theory and classical liberalism to techno-libertarianism and post-liberal thought. *You can't defend what you haven't understood how to attack.*

**Civics 101: Anatomy of Governance in the US**
What every government professional needs to know about how the system works — and is supposed to work.

**Policy and Governance as Response to Market and Alignment Failure**
Extends the conventional market-failure frame to a world of intelligent agents and misaligned institutional incentives.

---

## Toolbox: The analytical core

**Uncertainty for Practitioners**
This is our statistics course — but it isn't a statistics course. It's structured around one core reflex: *every observation is a sample from a distribution.* Probability, decision trees, causal inference, and risk are all built on top of that. Students leave with the habit, not just the tools.

**Systems Thinking**
Feedback loops, emergence, tipping points, leverage points. How to map a system before you try to fix it, and how to diagnose the unintended consequences of interventions. Includes agent-based modeling, AI-driven scenario planning, and real governance case studies: pandemic response, climate adaptation, financial oversight.

**Analytical Tools for Critical Policy-Making and Implementation**
Cost-benefit analysis, path dependence, simulation, and modeling for policy. The quantitative toolkit for when intuition isn't enough.

**Finding Out: Research Methods for the Practical Policy Activist**
How to carry out, understand, and critically assess the full range of methods. The goal: students who have deep respect for method as the foundation of credibility and accountability — and who know when to use which tool.

---

## Toolbox: The practical core

**Problem Solving Five Ways**
Five professional modes — rational analysis, creative innovation, engineering pragmatism, legal reasoning, political negotiation — each with its own logic of how problems are framed, what counts as a constraint, and what success looks like. Students learn to recognize which mode is operating in a room, deploy each deliberately, and collaborate across all five.

**Service Design for Government**
Effective governance isn't just policy — it's how services are experienced. Students work with a real government agency on a real service challenge: journey mapping, rapid prototyping, stakeholder engagement, feasible intervention design. *Thinking like a designer without becoming one.*

**Maxims and Mantras**
"Move fast and break things." "Culture eats strategy for breakfast." "Personnel is policy." Leadership axioms as entry points into serious analysis of organizations, power, and decision-making — and training in the rhetorical skills that actually move people.

**Building Regulatory Infrastructure to Secure Abundance**
How effective regulation *enables* markets, innovation, and prosperity — not just constrains them. Comparative cases: financial markets, labor, technology, environment, housing. The politics and craft of regulatory design.

---

## The [School Name] signature sequence

**Driving Quality and Value in Public Investment**
The program's foundational philosophy course. Built around an affirmative belief: there *are* skills, practices, structures, and institutions that enable governments to produce good outcomes for humans. This is the course that teaches students how we can "have nice things" — and why we so often don't. International comparative cases; a toolkit for producing public value.

**Translational Intervention and Implementation I → II → III**
The three-semester lab at the heart of the program.
- *Semester I:* Skills, background, and structured labs — the art of the possible
- *Semester II:* Supervised projects with real government partners
- *Semester III:* Independent team projects on problems students choose

This is where the curriculum becomes practice. Students leave with a track record of real work, not just a degree.

*These are the courses that make this program this program.*

## The invitation

This curriculum is a **bet** — on the skills and sensibilities that will matter in mid-21st century governance, made explicitly and defended openly rather than encoded silently in a list of required courses.

We are not trying to build a better version of an existing program. We are trying to build something that doesn't yet exist: a school that takes seriously the world its graduates will actually inhabit.

What we need from colleagues who want to be part of this:

- **Intellectual courage** — willingness to teach across and beyond your discipline
- **Problem-driven orientation** — the problem is the organizing principle, not the field
- **Appetite for iteration** — this curriculum will evolve as we learn what works

> "The challenge is two-sided. Figuring out what the new thing should look like is hard. Maybe harder is that we educators rarely agree on anything more than incremental change."

**We think you're the exception. That's why you're in this room.**
